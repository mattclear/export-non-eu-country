1. Overview

This guide gives details of how to assess the value of goods for import, ex- customs warehouse and export procedures. It is not exhaustive however, and importers/exporters are therefore advised to obtain and study Notice 252 and Notice 702.

2. Imports

2.1 For ad valorem Customs Duty purposes

The methods of assessing value to be declared on import (and ex-customs warehouse) entries of goods subject to ad valorem duty 1 are laid down in EC Regulations 2913/92, 2454/93 and 1762/95.

This value is arrived at by applying one of the methods listed below, in the sequence shown:

  1. Valuation method 1 - the transaction value of the imported goods (the price paid or payable for these goods).
  2. Valuation method 2 - the transaction value of identical goods (goods which are the same in all respects including physical characteristics, quality and reputation).
  3. Valuation method 3 - the transaction value of similar goods (goods which have like characteristics and component materials which enable them to perform the same functions).
  4. Value method 4 - the deductive method (the value derived from the selling price of the imported goods or identical similar goods in the EU).
  5. Valuation method 5 - the computed value (the value based on the built-up cost of the imported goods).
  6. Valuation method 6 - the fall-back method (the value based on reasonable means constituent with valuation principles).

The order of application of methods 4 and 5 can be reversed at the importer’s request.

2.2 Imports of fresh fruit and vegetables

When produce is imported on consignment, where they are in operation an importer may use a simplified procedure (SPVs). Full details are in sections 9 and 42 of Notice 252.

Produce imported under the entry price system must only use one of the three methods detailed in section 10 of Notice 252.

2.3 Imports free of charge

It is only possible to use method 1 if the goods were previously purchased by the consignor and the importer can produce evidence attesting to that purchase. The goods must also at the time of the previous sale be intended for export to the EU.

Details are in section 12 of Notice 252.

2.4 Delivery costs

When imported goods are subject to ad valorem Customs Duty the cost of transport, insurance, loading or handling up to the place of introduction into the customs territory of the EU must be included in the value for customs purposes. Delivery costs incurred after introduction are not liable to ad valorem Customs Duty if they are separately distinguished.

For imports by air, there are set percentages of aircraft costs for the proportion of the flight outside EU airspace.

Further details are given in sections 17 and 46 of Notice 252. Set percentage deductions are not available for other forms of transport.

2.5 Value expressed in a foreign currency

Where a value is expressed in a foreign currency it must be converted into sterling by using the monthly rate of exchange published by HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) and valid on the day that the import entry or with certain exceptions, the ex-customs warehouse entry (C259) is accepted.

Details of rates of exchange can be obtained from the local Customs Entry Processing Unit. At ports and airports served by HMRC computerised entry processing system a facility exists for this conversion to be undertaken automatically.

2.6 Further information

Further information about the valuation of goods for ad valorem Customs Duty purposes is contained in Notice 252.

2.7 Value for statistical purposes

For goods subject to ad valorem duty the value for Customs Duty purposes (as determined above) is to be taken as the statistical value.

For all other goods (ie goods free or exempted from duty including those imported from other member states of the European Union, and goods subject to a specific duty), the statistical value is to be determined in relation to the point at which the goods enter the UK.

This means that the value must include freight, insurance, commission and all other costs, charges and expenses incidental to the sale and delivery of the goods to the port or place of importation into the UK. Any duty or tax chargeable in the UK is excluded.

When the goods are re-imported after process or repair abroad the value to be declared must include the cost of the process or repair and the value of the goods when exported.

2.8 Value for VAT purposes

The value of imported goods for VAT purposes is their customs value plus, if not already included in the price:

  • all incidental expenses such as commission, packing, transport and insurance costs incurred up to the goods’ first destination in the UK
  • all such incidental costs where they result from transport to a further place of destination in the EU if that place is known at the time of importation
  • any Customs Duty or levy payable on importation into the UK
  • any excise duty or other charges payable on importation into the UK (except the VAT itself)

Where a value is expressed in a foreign currency it must be converted into sterling.

Find further information on VAT in general and the value for VAT purposes is given in VAT, Notice 252 and Notice 702.

2.9 Value for ad valorem excise duty purposes (cigarettes)

For the method of determining the ‘retail’ price for the purposes of assessing the ad valorem element of the excise duty on cigarettes, see paragraph excise duties, reliefs, drawbacks and allowances and paragraph 2.13 of Notice 476.

3. Exports

3.1 General

The statistical value for exported goods is represented by the cost of the goods to the purchaser abroad (or, if there is no sale, the price which the goods would fetch if sold to a purchaser abroad).

This includes the cost of packing, inland and coastal transport in the UK, dock dues, loading charges and all other costs, profits, charges and expenses (eg insurance and commission) incurred up to the point where the goods are deposited on board the exporting vessel or aircraft or at the land boundary of Northern Ireland.

3.2 Re-exported goods

For goods re-exported after process or repair in the UK, the value to be declared must include the charge for the process and the value of the goods when imported.

3.3 Goods subject to export Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) charges and/or refunds

Where goods are subject to a charge which is not included in the selling price invoiced to the customer abroad, the amount of the charge is to be added to the value invoiced for export statistical purposes.

Where a tax and refund are involved and the result is a net charge, the net charge is to be included in the value declared.

Where the result is a net refund, or where only a refund is involved, this is to be deducted to arrive at the value to be declared, unless the refund has already been excluded from the invoiced price.

3.4 Freight and insurance charges

In all cases, outwards sea or air freight and marine or air insurance should be excluded, and any cash or trade discounts granted to the purchaser abroad deducted.
